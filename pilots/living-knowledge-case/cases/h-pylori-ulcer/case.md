# Case Overview

## Working case title

**H. pylori and Peptic Ulcer Disease: from false rejection to clinical stabilization**

## Why it matters here

This case is included because it exposes a structural problem and a structural correction at the same time:

**a medically important claim can be resisted for years, then later rewrite practice once the evidence chain becomes unavoidable.**

For a long period, ulcer disease was not treated as a bacterium-driven disease in the dominant public and clinical imagination.
Later, the evidence base changed the judgment.
That change matters because it did not only revise a paper interpretation.
It changed diagnostics, treatment logic, and the meaning of cure.

## Pilot purpose

The purpose of this case is to test whether Knowledge OS can represent a claim as a living object with:

- a disputed initial formulation,
- attached evidence,
- explicit dissent,
- later verdict stabilization,
- a narrower descendant claim,
- and public-risk guardrails appropriate to a health-related case.

## Current object set

The current first object skeleton includes:

- **Claim C-0001** — the stronger causal and treatment-shifting claim
- **Claim C-0002** — the narrower clinical-path descendant claim
- **Evidence E-0001** — early observation and cultivation breakthrough
- **Evidence E-0002** — treatment response and recurrence-change evidence
- **Evidence E-0003** — consensus and guideline uptake
- **Dissent D-0001** — acid-environment plausibility objection
- **Dissent D-0002** — causality-versus-bystander objection
- **Dissent D-0003** — overgeneralization objection
- **Verdict V-0001** — working verdict on the stronger claim
- **Verdict V-0002** — working verdict on the narrower descendant claim

This is still not the full historical literature.
But it is dense enough to begin testing shell reuse and a second knowledge-fate pattern.

## Current thesis of the case

The case is expected to show at least five things:

1. A claim may be wrongly rejected before later stabilization.
2. A clinical reversal is still a governed knowledge lifecycle, not just a heroic anecdote.
3. Dissent should remain attached rather than disappearing after the verdict flips.
4. A stabilized claim can still narrow into a more careful descendant path.
5. A public case about health needs stronger public-risk guardrails than a low-stakes social-science controversy.
