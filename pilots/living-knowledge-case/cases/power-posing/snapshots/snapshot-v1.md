# Snapshot v1

## Title

**Power Posing: a living knowledge snapshot of a disputed scientific claim**

## Current visible state

### Original claim
**Status:** contested and significantly weakened

The original strong-form claim is that brief expansive postures can change hormone levels and affect risk-taking behavior.

### Descendant claim
**Status:** contested but still surviving as a weaker path

A weaker descendant claim remains in play: expansive posture may affect subjective feelings of power or confidence even if the stronger hormone-and-risk-taking claim does not hold.

---

## Why this case exists

This case exists because it shows a structural failure in the way modern knowledge is usually managed.

A paper can remain stable in public circulation while the status of its core claim changes dramatically.
In a static publication system, those later changes are scattered across replications, statements, critiques, blog posts, and memory.

In a living knowledge system, those later changes should be attached to the claim itself.

---

## The original claim

The original 2010 study by Carney, Cuddy, and Yap reported that high-power posing produced:

- higher testosterone,
- lower cortisol,
- greater feelings of power,
- and more tolerance for risk.

In the current pilot reading, this original phase is represented by:

- **C-0001** — the original strong-form claim
- **E-0001** — the initial publication and early support context

---

## What changed later

### 1. A larger replication challenge appeared
In 2015, Ranehill et al. published a large-sample replication challenge.
In the current pilot reading, this is represented by **D-0001**.

### 2. Support weakened from inside the original authorship chain
In 2016, Dana Carney publicly stated that she no longer believed the power-pose effects were real.
In the pilot, this is represented by **D-0002**.

### 3. The support literature itself came under methodological attack
Also in 2016, Simmons and Simonsohn's p-curve analysis challenged the evidentiary profile of the literature around the effect.
In the pilot, this is represented by **D-0003**.

### 4. The case split rather than ending cleanly
The original strong-form claim did not simply remain healthy or disappear.
Instead, the pilot models the case as splitting into a weaker descendant path represented by **C-0002**.

---

## Current pilot verdicts

### `V-0001`
The original strong-form claim should not be treated as stably supported.
It remains visible, but in a weakened and contested state.

### `V-0002`
The weaker descendant claim remains contested but more viable than the original strong-form claim.

---

## Why this is a living knowledge case

This snapshot is intentionally different from a normal paper summary.
It does not ask only, “What did the original article say?”
It asks:

- What was claimed?
- What evidence supported it?
- What dissent accumulated?
- What is the current visible judgment?
- Did the claim die, weaken, or split?

That is the transition from a static publication artifact to a living knowledge object.
