# Snapshot Section Layering v1

This note records the current **snapshot section layering rule** for the `power-posing` pilot.

It exists because `snapshot-v2.md` is no longer just a rough narrative note.
It now acts as the current **public homepage / release view** for the case.

That means it needs structure.
Not ornamental structure, but governed section structure.

> a snapshot should remain readable like a public page,
> while still being stable enough that its major reading surfaces do not drift silently over time.

---

## One-sentence ruling

Current ruling:

> **The snapshot is the public release-view layer.**
> **Its sections must keep judgment, neighborhood, change-history, reading-path, and included-object surfaces legible and distinct.**

Do not let it decay back into a single undifferentiated long page.

---

## Why this note exists now

The current pilot already has multiple public-facing layers:

- claim pages,
- source pages,
- and the main snapshot.

The snapshot has a different job from the other two.
It is not supposed to be:

- a raw object file,
- a claim-specific page,
- or a source-specific page.

It is the current public release view over the case as a whole.

That means the snapshot must hold together several reading jobs at once:

- tell the reader what this page is,
- expose the current visible judgment,
- explain why the case matters,
- show the original claim neighborhood,
- explain what changed later,
- and provide a stable reading path into deeper layers.

Without a section grammar, this surface will drift fastest because it is the most narrative and the most public.

---

## Layer split

### Layer A. governed object / protocol layers

These layers hold:

- claim objects,
- evidence objects,
- dissent objects,
- verdict objects,
- verdict grammar,
- status legend,
- and protocol grammar.

These layers carry the governed substrate.

### Layer B. claim / source page layers

These layers restore object- and source-specific public readability.

They help a reader go deeper into one governed surface at a time.

### Layer C. snapshot release-view layer

This layer gives the reader the current whole-case public entry surface.

Its job is not to replace the governed substrate.
Its job is to stage it into a legible release view.

---

## Current minimum snapshot obligations

At the current stage, `snapshot-v2.md` should expose at least the following sections.

### 1. `Title`

This section gives the reader the visible public title of the current release view.

### 2. `What this page is`

This section tells the reader what kind of page they are looking at.

Minimum expectations:

- it should state that the page is the public homepage or release view,
- and it should distinguish itself from full archive or full live graph.

### 3. `Current visible judgment`

This section exposes the public-facing judgment surface.

Minimum expectations:

- it should visibly link to `status-legend-v1.md`,
- it should visibly link to `verdict-grammar-v1.md`,
- it should include the current state of the original claim,
- it should include the current state of the descendant claim.

### 4. `Why this case matters`

This section explains the structural significance of the case.

Minimum expectations:

- enough prose to explain why the case is not just one more paper dispute,
- and why living knowledge handling matters here.

### 5. `Original claim neighborhood`

This section gives the reader an explicit handle on the early support surface and starting claim package.

Minimum expectations:

- original claim route,
- early support object route,
- visible canonical source ids or source routes.

### 6. `What changed later`

This section stages the later challenge-and-lineage developments.

Minimum expectations:

- the main empirical challenge,
- the internal collapse of support,
- the methodological challenge,
- and the weaker descendant route.

This section is the snapshot’s main change-history surface.

### 7. `Snapshot reading path`

This section gives the reader a deliberate route into deeper layers.

Minimum expectations:

- at least one path into status legend or verdict grammar,
- at least one path into claim pages,
- at least one path into object layer or timeline,
- at least one path into source or reference layers.

### 8. `Included objects`

This section makes the current governed release set visible.

Minimum expectations:

- the reader should be able to see which governed objects are explicitly included in this snapshot surface.

These are not decorative sections.
They are the minimum release-view structure of the current snapshot layer.

---

## What this note does **not** require

This note does **not** require that the snapshot:

- duplicate every claim-page section,
- duplicate every source-page section,
- become a full graph explorer,
- or freeze every paragraph of narrative prose.

The goal is not maximal rigidity.
The goal is durable public structure.

---

## Current design rule

When there is tension between:

- making the snapshot feel fluid and essay-like,
- and keeping the release-view structure stable enough to audit,

the preferred move is:

1. keep the section grammar stable,
2. then evolve the prose inside those sections.

Do **not** let the snapshot regress into a beautiful but structurally drifting page.
That drift belongs to draft notes, not to the public release view.

---

## Practical verdict

So the practical verdict is:

- **the snapshot should remain the structured public release-view layer**
- **its judgment, neighborhood, change-history, reading-path, and included-object surfaces should remain explicit**
- **the current pilot should stabilize this section grammar rather than let snapshot prose drift without checks**

That is the right architecture for the current stage.
