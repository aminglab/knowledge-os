# Claim C-0001

## Summary

This is the original strong-form power-posing claim associated with the 2010 paper by Carney, Cuddy, and Yap.
The core proposition is that holding expansive, high-power body postures for a short period can change hormone levels and influence behavioral decision-making.

## Working formulation

A concise working formulation of the original claim is:

> Two minutes of expansive, high-power posture can increase testosterone, decrease cortisol, and increase risk-taking behavior.

## Why this object matters

This is the claim whose later lifecycle demonstrates the difference between static publication and living governance.
It was not merely published. It was amplified, challenged, weakened, and later split.

## Early support context

The original study is associated in this pilot with:

- publication year: **2010**,
- sample size: **42 participants**,
- and reported significance in the original presentation context: **p < 0.05**.

Those details matter because they shaped the first phase of the claim's public legitimacy.

## Working assumptions

- short-duration embodied posture may affect psychological state,
- hormone changes can serve as part of the relevant mechanism,
- a laboratory intervention of this scale can produce measurable downstream effects.

## Current pilot reading

This claim should remain distinct from later weaker descendant claims about subjective feeling alone.
The pilot therefore treats the original strong claim and the later retreated claim as different objects in the same lineage.
